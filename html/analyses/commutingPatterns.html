Overview

The analysis of commuting patterns focuses on movement of residents and workers between their residences and workplaces. This analysis helps inform planners about both commuting options and patterns.

Data Sources

Source Description
ACS Data Census data is helpful for an overview of commuting options and resident characteristics. Specifically, Table B08301 from the American Community Survey (ACS) provides an overview of the means of transportation utilized by residents of a specific area. To learn more about ACS data and how to pull it, refer to the Existing Conditions page.
Census OnTheMap Census OnTheMap is a web-interface produced by the Census Bureau that provides a detailed overview of, given a geography, the number of people who live and work there (residents and workers), the number of people who live but don't work there (out-commuters), and the number of people who don't live there but do work there (in-commuters). Census OnTheMap is a helpful tool to provide an overview of the commuting patterns for a given geography.
Longitudinal Employer-Household Dynamics (LEHD) The Longitudinal Employer-Household Dynamics data is what Census OnTheMap is based on. LEHD provides a dataset known as LODES (LEHD Origin-Destination Employment Statistics), which provide granular data on the number of jobs in each Census Block, where those workers are coming from, and what kinds of jobs they are working (industries, wages, etc).

Using Census OnTheMap

  • Visit Census OnTheMap.
  • Use the search bar on the left to look for your area of interest. Ensure that you select the right geography. For example, searching for "los angeles" returns several results. In this example, we are concerned with "Los Angeles city". Click on it to select it, then click on "Perform analysis on Selection Area".
  • This opens up a popup asking for several specifications.
  • The "Analysis Type" has several options, of which "Destination" and "Inflow/Outflow" are commonly used.
  • Destination: Shows the home or work destinations of workers employed or living in the selected study area. You choose the geographic destination type (counties, cities, tracts, etc.) from a dropdown. In practice, this tells you where the workers in your study area are commuting to — or, flipped to a home area, where residents are commuting from.
  • Inflow/Outflow: Shows the count and characteristics of worker flows into, out of, and within the selected study area. This breaks the workforce into three buckets: workers who live and work inside the area (internal), workers who live outside but work inside (in-commuters), and workers who live inside but work outside (out-commuters). It's the standard analysis for understanding whether an area is a net job importer or exporter.

Using LEHD/LODES Data

LODES data offers the most detailed analysis of commuting patterns, but is also the most complex to analyze. LODES offers three analysis types - Origin-Destination (OD), Workplace Area Characteristics (WAC), and Resident Area Characterstics (RAC).

  • Origin-Destination (OD): Unique pairs of home and destination Census Blocks showing the number of workers that commute from any given Census Block to another Census Block for work.
  • Workplace Area Characteristics (WAC): Job totals by workplace Census Block, showing, for any given Census Block, the characteristics of the jobs that are worked in that Block.
  • Resident Area Characteristics (RAC): Job totals by home Census Block, showing, for any given Census Block, how many workers live there, regardless of where they might work.

Refer to the LODES Technical Documentation for a detailed explanation of file structures and contents.
